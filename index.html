The Era of Luck

For most of the 20th century, popular American board games were defined by Dice Rolling. You rolled, you moved, and the game decided your fate. Think Risk, Trouble, or Monopoly. While fun, these games offered little agency.

The Eurogame Revolution

This began to change in the 1990s, dubbed the ‚ÄúEurogame‚Äù revolution, featuring games that were more strategy-heavy. One of its prime examples is Catan, one of the first European games that succeeded in the United States, published in 1995. In Catan, new mechanics like Hand Management (playing cards from your hand at the right time) gave players more control. This ushered in a whole new era of board games, one that not only took luck, but skill.

The Golden Age

In the past twenty years, board games have exploded in popularity. The Internet has given players the ability to try out new board games at minimal cost; crowdfunding platforms like Kickstarter have lowered the barrier of entry for board game designers. All of this has resulted in what is now called the ‚Äúgolden age of board games.‚Äù With the board game industry flourishing, designers have revitalized board games with new mechanics. For example, these new mechanics include Area Majority and Influence (controlling areas on a map), Deck Building (constructing your own deck as you play), and Cooperative Games (winning or losing together).

Defining "Weight"

BoardGameGeek uses a metric called Weight (1-5) to measure complexity.

1.0 - 2.0: Party games like Uno or Codenames. Simple rules, quick fun.
3.0+: Strategy games like Scythe or Terraforming Mars. Complex rules, deep planning.

This chart shows weight on a scale of 1-5 versus the average user rating for a board game on a scale of 1-10, with each point representing a game.

The Enthusiast Effect

On Board Game Geek, "heavier" (more complicated) games tend to have higher ratings. This is probably because the people who play them invest time and effort, leading to a more dedicated fanbase.

However, most games that are created tend to be on the "lighter" end of the scale, with their weight being around 1 to 3.

The Newer, the Better

Modern games range in a wide range of complexity, but they tend to fare better. Newer games -- games published after 2015 -- are highlighted in red. Notice how they are higher rated than older games. Clearly, years of design have paid off -- we truly are in a golden age of board game design.
